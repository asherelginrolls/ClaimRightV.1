## Chunk 1: Precedent — Piecemeal Document Requests (IOB/MUM/2022/HI/00147)

Case ID: IOB/MUM/2022/HI/00147. Insurance Ombudsman, Mumbai. Insurer: Star Health and Allied Insurance Company Ltd. Rejection category: documentation_incomplete. Claim amount: ₹1,85,000. Date of award: March 2023. Facts: The complainant was hospitalised for cardiac surgery and filed a reimbursement claim. The insurer rejected the claim on three separate occasions, each time citing the absence of a different document: first requesting the discharge summary, then hospital bills, and finally a treating doctor certificate, over a period of forty-five days. The complainant submitted all documents requested in each round. The insurer ultimately repudiated the claim alleging incomplete documentation. Holding: The Insurance Ombudsman held that the insurer's conduct in issuing three sequential document requests over forty-five days was a clear violation of the IRDAI Master Circular on Health Insurance dated 29 May 2024, which prohibits insurers from making piecemeal document requests and mandates that all required documents be demanded in a single written communication at the first instance. The sequential requests were found to be a dilatory tactic. Outcome: The ombudsman directed Star Health to settle the claim of ₹1,85,000 in full and to pay interest at 2% per month from the date of the first document submission, as mandated under the IRDAI Master Circular on Health Insurance. Key holding: Piecemeal document requests constitute a violation of IRDAI health insurance regulations and entitle the claimant to full settlement with interest.

## Chunk 2: Precedent — Cashless Pre-Authorization Delay (IOB/DEL/2023/HI/00089)

Case ID: IOB/DEL/2023/HI/00089. Insurance Ombudsman, New Delhi. Insurer: New India Assurance Company Ltd. Rejection category: cashless_denial. Claim amount: ₹92,000. Date of award: September 2023. Facts: The complainant was admitted to a network hospital for an emergency appendectomy. The hospital submitted a cashless pre-authorization request to the insurer's TPA (third party administrator) at 11:00 AM. The TPA did not respond within the one-hour period prescribed by the IRDAI Master Circular on Health Insurance dated 29 May 2024. By 3:00 PM, having received no response, the hospital asked the complainant to arrange for funds on a private-pay basis. The complainant settled the bill and filed a reimbursement claim, which the insurer later partially settled at a reduced rate citing network hospital rate caps. The complainant filed a complaint before the Insurance Ombudsman. Holding: The Insurance Ombudsman held that the insurer's failure to respond to the cashless pre-authorization request within one hour, as required by the IRDAI Master Circular on Health Insurance, constituted a material breach of the insurer's regulatory obligation. The insurer's delay in pre-authorization forced the complainant into a private-pay arrangement; the insurer could not thereafter apply network rate caps to a situation caused by its own non-compliance. Outcome: The ombudsman directed New India Assurance to settle the full claim of ₹92,000 without applying network rate cap deductions. Key holding: An insurer that fails to decide a cashless pre-authorization request within one hour cannot subsequently invoke rate cap provisions against the claimant.

## Chunk 3: Precedent — Pre-Existing Condition After Moratorium (IOB/BLR/2023/HI/00312)

Case ID: IOB/BLR/2023/HI/00312. Insurance Ombudsman, Bengaluru. Insurer: HDFC ERGO General Insurance Company Ltd. Rejection category: pre_existing_condition. Claim amount: ₹3,40,000. Date of award: November 2023. Facts: The complainant held a health insurance policy with the insurer for a continuous period of sixty-seven months prior to hospitalisation for Type 2 diabetes-related complications. The insurer repudiated the claim on the ground that diabetes was a pre-existing disease (PED) that the complainant had failed to disclose at the time of policy inception. The complainant contended that the policy had been in force for more than sixty months and that the moratorium under the Protection of Policyholders' Interests (PPOI) Master Circular dated 5 September 2024 barred repudiation on PED grounds. Holding: The Insurance Ombudsman held that the PPOI Master Circular dated 5 September 2024 establishes an absolute sixty-month moratorium on repudiation of claims on the ground of pre-existing disease non-disclosure, after which no insurer may repudiate a claim on that ground save in cases of established and documented fraud. The insurer's repudiation was set aside. The insurer had produced no evidence of fraud. Outcome: The ombudsman directed HDFC ERGO to settle the claim of ₹3,40,000 in full within thirty days. Key holding: After sixty continuous months of coverage, an insurer is barred from repudiating a health insurance claim on pre-existing disease or non-disclosure grounds unless it establishes fraud by clear and convincing evidence.
