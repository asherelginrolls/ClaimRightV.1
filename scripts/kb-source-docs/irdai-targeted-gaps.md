## Chunk 1: Experimental, Investigational and Unproven Treatment — IRDAI Position

IRDAI regulations under the Master Circular on Health Insurance Business dated 29 May 2024 address the question of experimental, investigational, and unproven treatments. An insurer may not reject a health insurance claim on the sole ground that a treatment is "experimental", "investigational", "unproven", or "non-standard" if: (a) the treatment is included in IRDAI's mandated list of advanced and technological treatments (including robotic surgery, stereotactic radiosurgery, immunotherapy, oral chemotherapy, HIFU, deep brain stimulation, intravitreal injections, bronchial thermoplasty, and others enumerated in the Master Circular); or (b) the treatment is supported by a certificate from the treating specialist certifying medical necessity and that the treatment is consistent with current medical practice; or (c) the treatment has been approved or accepted by a recognised national or international medical body. The burden of proof lies on the insurer to establish that the treatment is genuinely outside the scope of covered treatments — not on the policyholder to prove it is established. Rejecting a claim as "experimental" or "unproven" without reference to the policy wording, the IRDAI permitted treatment list, and contemporaneous medical evidence constitutes arbitrary repudiation. The Insurance Ombudsman has consistently held that treating doctor certificates carry significant weight in overturning experimental treatment exclusions.

## Chunk 2: Grievance Redressal Officer (GRO) — Insurer Obligations and 15-Day Response Requirement

Under the IRDAI Master Circular on Health Insurance Business dated 29 May 2024, every insurer is required to establish and maintain a Grievance Redressal Officer (GRO) system to handle policyholder complaints. The GRO mechanism operates as follows. A policyholder who is dissatisfied with a claim decision may submit a written complaint to the insurer's designated GRO. The insurer is obligated to respond in writing to the complainant within fifteen days of receipt of the complaint, with a reasoned response either accepting, partially accepting, or rejecting the grievance. If the insurer fails to respond within fifteen days, or if the response is unsatisfactory, the policyholder has the right to escalate the matter to the IRDAI Integrated Grievance Management System (IGMS) — also called the Bima Bharosa portal — at bimabharosa.irdai.gov.in, or directly to the Insurance Ombudsman having jurisdiction over the insurer's office. The insurer must maintain records of all GRO complaints and their resolutions, and must report grievance data to IRDAI periodically. A pattern of non-response or inadequate response by the GRO is treated as regulatory non-compliance. The fifteen-day GRO response window is a binding obligation, not a target: failure to respond within this window gives the policyholder standing to escalate without further notice to the insurer.

## Chunk 3: Third-Party Administrator (TPA) — IRDAI Obligations and Claim Processing Duties

A Third Party Administrator (TPA) is an entity licensed by IRDAI under the IRDAI (Third Party Administrators — Health Services) Regulations to process health insurance claims on behalf of one or more insurers. Under the IRDAI Master Circular on Health Insurance Business dated 29 May 2024, the insurer remains fully responsible for all actions, decisions, and obligations of its TPA. A TPA cannot independently repudiate a claim; all repudiations must be authorised by the insurer and communicated to the policyholder by the insurer (not the TPA). A claim rejection or denial issued by a TPA without explicit insurer authorisation is not a valid repudiation. The insurer cannot use the TPA as a shield to avoid regulatory obligations: delays attributable to TPA processing are treated as delays by the insurer for the purpose of the 30-day settlement deadline and cashless pre-authorization timelines. The IRDAI requires insurers to monitor TPA performance and to ensure that the TPA processes claims within the timelines mandated by the Master Circular. Policyholders dealing with TPA-level rejections should direct their formal complaint to the insurer's GRO, not the TPA, as the insurer is the responsible regulatory entity.
