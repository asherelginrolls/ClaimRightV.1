## Chunk 1: Section 2(7) — Definition of Deficiency in Service

Section 2(7) of the Consumer Protection Act 2019 (Act No. 35 of 2019) defines "deficiency" as follows: "deficiency" means any fault, imperfection, shortcoming or inadequacy in the quality, nature and manner of performance which is required to be maintained by or under any law for the time being in force or has been undertaken to be performed by a person in pursuance of a contract or otherwise in relation to any service, and includes any act of negligence or omission or commission by such person which causes loss or injury to the consumer; and any deliberate withholding of relevant information by such person to the consumer. In the context of health insurance, deficiency in service includes unreasonable repudiation of a valid claim, failure to respond within statutory timeframes prescribed by IRDAI, issuing arbitrary or reasoned rejection letters, and failure to follow the grievance redressal procedure mandated by IRDAI regulations. A finding of deficiency in service by the District Commission entitles the complainant to compensation and other remedies under Section 39 of the Act.

## Chunk 2: Section 2(11) — Definition of Unfair Trade Practice

Section 2(11) of the Consumer Protection Act 2019 defines "unfair trade practice" as a trade practice which, for the purpose of promoting the sale, use, or supply of any goods or for the provision of any service, adopts any unfair method or unfair or deceptive practice including: (i) making any false representation about the nature, characteristics, or suitability of goods or services; (ii) falsely representing that services are of a particular standard, quality, or grade; (iii) permitting publication of any advertisement whether in any newspaper or otherwise, for the sale or supply of goods or services at a bargain price when there is no reasonable prospect of the advertised goods being available at that price. In the insurance context, unfair trade practices have been held by consumer courts to include misrepresentation of policy coverage at the time of sale, failure to inform the insured of waiting period or exclusion clauses in clear and unambiguous terms, and selectively applying policy clauses in a manner that is adverse to the insured but was not communicated at the time of purchase.

## Chunk 3: Section 2(46) — Definition of Service Including Insurance

Section 2(46) of the Consumer Protection Act 2019 defines "service" broadly to include insurance services. The provision reads: "service" means service of any description which is made available to potential users and includes, but not limited to, the provision of facilities in connection with banking, financing, insurance, transport, processing, supply of electrical or other energy, telecom, boarding or lodging or both, housing construction, entertainment, amusement or the purveying of news or other information, but does not include the rendering of any service free of charge or under a contract of personal service. The explicit inclusion of insurance in the definition of "service" under the Consumer Protection Act 2019 means that all insurers are subject to consumer protection jurisdiction in India. A policyholder who has paid premium for a health insurance policy is unambiguously a "consumer" within the meaning of Section 2(7) of the Act, and any deficiency in the settlement or rejection of a health insurance claim constitutes actionable deficiency in service.

## Chunk 4: Section 35 — Filing Complaint Before District Commission

Section 35 of the Consumer Protection Act 2019 governs the filing of complaints before the District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission. A complaint relating to any goods sold or delivered or any service provided may be filed before the District Commission by: (a) the consumer; (b) any recognised consumer association, whether or not such consumer is a member; (c) the Central Government or any State Government; (d) one or more consumers, where there are numerous consumers having the same interest, with the leave of the District Commission. The complaint must be filed in the District Commission within whose jurisdiction the complainant resides or personally works for gain, or where the opposite party or each of the opposite parties resides or carries on business, has a branch office, or personally works for gain. The pecuniary jurisdiction of the District Commission is for disputes not exceeding one crore rupees in value. The complainant need not hire a legal practitioner and may appear in person; the proceeding is summary in nature.

## Chunk 5: Section 69 — Limitation Period for Filing Consumer Complaint

Section 69 of the Consumer Protection Act 2019 prescribes the limitation period for filing a consumer complaint. Sub-section (1) provides that the District Commission, the State Commission, or the National Commission shall not admit a complaint unless it is filed within two years from the date on which the cause of action has arisen. In an insurance context, the cause of action typically arises on the date of the insurer's final rejection of the claim or the date of the insurer's last communication denying the grievance. Sub-section (2) provides an important saving provision: notwithstanding sub-section (1), a complaint may be entertained after the two-year period if the complainant satisfies the Commission that there was sufficient cause for not filing the complaint within such period, provided that no such complaint shall be entertained unless the Commission records its reasons for condoning the delay in writing. This two-year limitation period runs concurrently with (and is independent of) the one-year window for approaching the Insurance Ombudsman under Rule 13 of the Insurance Ombudsman Rules 2017.

## Chunk 6: Section 39 — Orders the District Commission May Pass

Section 39 of the Consumer Protection Act 2019 sets out the orders that a District Commission may pass if it is satisfied that the goods complained of suffer from any defect or the services complained of suffer from any deficiency. The District Commission may direct the opposite party to: (a) remove the defect in the goods or deficiency in the service; (b) replace the defective goods or re-perform the service; (c) return to the complainant the price, or the charges paid for the service; (d) pay such amount as may be awarded as compensation to the consumer for any loss or injury suffered by the consumer due to the negligence of the opposite party; (e) pay such amount not less than five per cent of the value of the disputed goods or services as punitive damages in such circumstances as the Commission may deem fit; (f) cease and desist from the unfair trade practice or restrictive trade practice; (g) pay adequate costs to the complainant. In insurance claim disputes, the District Commission regularly awards: (i) the full insured claim amount; (ii) interest on the delayed or withheld payment; (iii) compensation for mental agony and harassment; and (iv) costs. Awards up to ₹1 crore fall within District Commission jurisdiction.
