## Chunk 1: Waiting Period Rules — IRDAI Health Insurance Regulations 2024

The Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI), through the IRDAI (Insurance Products) Regulations 2024 and the Master Circular on Health Insurance Business dated 29 May 2024, has prescribed specific rules governing waiting periods in health insurance policies. The initial waiting period for any health insurance policy shall not exceed thirty days from the date of commencement of the policy, except for claims arising from accidents for which no waiting period shall apply. For specific listed illnesses, the maximum waiting period permissible is forty-eight months from the date of first enrollment. For pre-existing diseases (PED), the maximum waiting period shall not exceed thirty-six months from the date of commencement of the policy for individual policies. Upon expiry of the applicable waiting period, the insurer is bound to cover the condition without exception. An insurer rejecting a claim on the ground that a waiting period is still in force must demonstrate with documentary evidence that the specific illness or condition falls within the declared waiting period list, that the waiting period has not yet expired as on the date of admission, and that proper disclosure of the waiting period was made to the policyholder at the time of policy issuance. Failure to disclose waiting period conditions in the Customer Information Sheet (CIS) and policy schedule constitutes a regulatory violation under the Master Circular.

## Chunk 2: Policy Exclusion Clause — Disclosure and Contra Proferentem

Under the IRDAI Master Circular on Health Insurance Business dated 29 May 2024, all exclusion clauses in a health insurance policy must meet the following mandatory standards. Every exclusion must be disclosed clearly and specifically in: (i) the policy wording document, in plain language; (ii) the Customer Information Sheet (CIS) provided at the time of issuance; and (iii) the insurer's product brochure and sales literature. The IRDAI has prescribed that exclusions must be worded in simple, unambiguous English (or the local language of the policyholder where required), and that any ambiguity in the exclusion clause must be resolved in favour of the policyholder. This is consistent with the established legal principle of contra proferentem, which Indian courts and consumer forums have consistently applied to insurance contracts: where a contractual clause is capable of two interpretations, the interpretation adverse to the party that drafted the contract (the insurer) shall not prevail. An insurer invoking an exclusion clause must demonstrate: (a) the specific exclusion relied upon and its exact wording in the policy schedule; (b) that the exclusion was clearly disclosed to the policyholder at the time of sale; (c) the documented medical or factual basis for the applicability of the exclusion to the specific claim; and (d) that no alternate reasonable interpretation of the clause would cover the claim. A blanket or generic invocation of an exclusion without specific application to the diagnosed condition and treatment is not a valid ground for rejection under IRDAI regulations.

## Chunk 3: Advanced and Technological Treatments — Mandatory Coverage Under IRDAI

The IRDAI Master Circular on Health Insurance Business dated 29 May 2024 expressly provides that insurers shall endeavour to cover technological advancements and treatments in their health insurance products. The Master Circular enumerates specific advanced treatment modalities that health insurance policies must include and that insurers cannot exclude or decline to cover on the grounds of being experimental, investigational, or non-standard. The listed treatments that must be covered include: uterine artery embolization (UAE) and high-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU); balloon sinuplasty; deep brain stimulation; oral chemotherapy; immunotherapy using monoclonal antibody injections; intravitreal injections; robotic surgeries; stereotactic radiosurgeries (including Gamma Knife, CyberKnife); bronchial thermoplasty; vaporisation of the prostate using green laser or holmium laser; intraoperative neurological monitoring (IONM); and hematopoietic stem cells for bone marrow transplant for haematological conditions. The IRDAI has further mandated that any treatment using advanced technology, consistent with contemporary medical practice and supported by the treating physician, shall be considered eligible for coverage. An insurer rejecting a claim for any listed treatment as "experimental", "investigational", or "not a standard treatment" is acting contrary to the explicit mandate of the IRDAI Master Circular. The treating doctor's certificate attesting to the medical necessity and appropriateness of the procedure carries significant evidentiary weight before the Insurance Ombudsman and consumer courts.

## Chunk 4: Claim Repudiation — PPOI Requirements for Non-Disclosure and Misrepresentation

The Protection of Policyholders' Interests (PPOI) Master Circular dated 5 September 2024 sets out the conditions under which an insurer may repudiate a health insurance claim on the grounds of material non-disclosure, misrepresentation, or suppression of a material fact at the time of policy purchase. Under the PPOI Master Circular, a repudiation based on alleged non-disclosure or misrepresentation is valid only if: (a) the non-disclosed or misrepresented fact was "material" — i.e., it would have affected the insurer's decision to issue the policy or the premium charged; (b) the insurer can demonstrate through contemporaneous underwriting evidence that the specific fact was sought at the time of proposal and that the proposer's answer was false or incomplete; and (c) the insurer notifies the policyholder in writing with specific facts supporting the allegation of misrepresentation. The PPOI Master Circular further provides that after sixty continuous months of policy coverage, no repudiation on the ground of non-disclosure or misrepresentation of a pre-existing condition shall be permissible, unless the insurer establishes fraud. This sixty-month moratorium is an absolute bar: the insurer bears the burden of proving fraud by clear and convincing evidence. Silence, medical history inconsistencies, or subsequent discovery of a pre-existing condition are not evidence of fraud for this purpose. Any repudiation alleging non-disclosure must be reviewed and approved by the insurer's Claims Review Committee (CRC) or Policy Management Committee (PMC) before being communicated to the policyholder.
